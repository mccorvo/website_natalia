# Sources and research notes

This file gives Codex/developers the evidence base behind the product rules. Do not display this entire file to end users; use it to preserve traceability.

## Polish taste logic

- Culture.pl notes that Polish cuisine has a healthier side and can be built around fermentation, dairy, groats and vegetables.
- Fermented vegetables such as dill pickles and sauerkraut are described as omnipresent in Polish cuisine.
- Żur/żurek and barszcz use fermentation/sourness as part of the taste profile.
- Kefir and soured milk are common in Polish/Eastern European food culture.
- Groats/kasza, including buckwheat and barley, are important in Polish cuisine.
- Twaróg/quark/cottage cheese is central to Polish food and useful as a protein source.

## Nutrition/allergen rules

- Use EU/FSAI 14 allergen categories.
- Use EU thresholds for “source of protein” and “high protein”.
- Use EU thresholds for fibre claims if fibre data is available.
- Avoid vague “healthy” claims.

## Nutrition data sources

- USDA FoodData Central: public-domain/CC0 food composition data.
- Open Food Facts: useful for packaged products, but community-contributed and not guaranteed complete/accurate. Use with caution.

## Availability in Ireland

- Tesco Ireland has a Polish Groceries category with examples including sour cream, kefir, sauerkraut, pickled cucumbers and Polish sausages.
- Polonez is a specialist Polish/Eastern European shop chain with stores across Ireland and the UK.
- Always render availability as indicative, not guaranteed.

## Image policy

- Wikimedia Commons is preferred because file pages expose author/license/source metadata.
- Accept only public domain/free-licensed assets that allow reuse.
- Render attribution for CC BY, CC BY-SA and GFDL/CC BY-SA assets.
- Do not use random recipe blog photos or Pinterest/paid stock images.
